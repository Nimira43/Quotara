# CSS - Utility First V Component Based

## Utility‑First CSS: Tailwind and the Art of Writing CSS Without Writing CSS

Utility‑first CSS is what happens when someone looks at traditional styling and says,  
“You know what? I’m tired. Let’s just put everything in the HTML and hope for the best.”

Enter Tailwind.

Instead of writing CSS files like a responsible adult, you slap classes like  
`flex`, `gap-4`, `bg-fuchsia-700`, and `hover:rotate-12` directly onto your markup  
until your `<div>` looks like it’s trying to file a tax return.

### The Good

- It’s fast.  
- It’s consistent.  
- It makes you feel like a wizard casting layout spells.

### The Bad

Your HTML becomes so verbose it starts to resemble a ransom note.

### The Verdict

If you enjoy speed, consistency, and the thrill of writing 17 classes on one line,  
Tailwind is your friend.  
If you prefer “separation of concerns,” you may need a lie‑down.

## Component‑Based CSS: Because Sometimes You Want Your Styles to Behave

Component‑based CSS is the opposite of utility‑first.  
Instead of stuffing your HTML like a Christmas turkey, you tuck your styles neatly  
inside components using CSS Modules, Sass partials, or styled‑components.

It’s organised.  
It’s modular.  
It’s the KonMari method of styling.

### The Good

- Styles stay where they belong.  
- Components feel self‑contained and tidy.  
- Large apps don’t collapse into a screaming CSS nightmare.

### The Bad

You’ll spend half your time wondering whether a style lives in  
`Button.module.css`, `button.scss`, `StyledButton.js`, or the void.

### The Verdict

Perfect for big projects.  
Perfect for teams.  
Perfect for people who enjoy structure and don’t want their CSS to unionise.
